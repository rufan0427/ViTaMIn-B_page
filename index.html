ViTaMIn-B: Learning Bi-Manual Contact-Rich Tasks Through Robot-Free Visuo-Tactile Manipulation Interface

1Tsinghua University, 2University of California, Berkeley
* Equal contribution, † Corresponding author

Abstract

In this paper, we present ViTaMIn-B, a portable handheld system specifically designed for collecting visuo-tactile demonstrations in contact-rich bimanual manipulation tasks. While existing handheld data collection devices have made substantial progress, most focus on single-arm operations or lack integrated tactile sensing capabilities. To address the growing need for bimanual manipulation data, the system introduces key improvements: (1) novel compliant tactile sensors with high-resolution cameras that can perceive bidirectional contact signals and withstand large tangential forces, (2) lightweight and ergonomic mechanical design achieved by eliminating onboard computing modules and simplifying hardware architecture, reducing operator fatigue during extended data collection sessions, and (3) specialized bimanual coordination with both hands' poses captured directly within the same coordinate system and strict time synchronization across all sensing modalities. Comprehensive user studies demonstrate efficient operation across different user experience levels, with even inexperienced users capable of collecting high-quality data with minimal training. We validate the effectiveness of the collected multimodal data by training policies on challenging bimanual tasks, showing that incorporating tactile feedback substantially improves success rates compared to vision-only approaches. The results indicate that ViTaMIn-B advances portable data collection by providing a more accessible and specialized solution for bimanual visuo-tactile demonstration collection.

Hardware Design

We maintain the ArUco marker-based gripper width detection approach, with two markers providing real-time width measurement up to a maximum grasping width of 8 cm.

However, two key improvements were introduced: we oriented the GoPro camera downward such that the gripper’s working area is centered within the camera’s field of view, thereby capturing richer visual information. More importantly, we redesigned the mechanical structure to achieve a lighter and more ergonomic device that enhances usability during extended data collection sessions.



Tactile Sensor

Structural Composition

  • TPU Frame: Flexible metamaterial structure for shape adaptation
  • PVC Film: 2mm transparent film for load protection
  • Contact Layer (Fingertip & Skin): Silicone base, reflective coating, black overlay
  • Multi-color LED Array: Circular RGB configuration for deformation visualization
  • Fingertip Casing: The mechanical interface connecting the fingertip’s contact layer to the TPU frame
  • RGB Camera: 640×480 resolution at 30 fps for internal monitoring

Fabrication Process

Manufacturing Technique: All structural components were produced using additive manufacturing, with TPU frames 3D-printed and other parts created via photocuring resin printing.

Skin Component Preparation

PVC film inserted into mold cavity, transparent silicone adhesive applied around edges, and approximately 10g of degassed transparent silicone gel (Wacker Elastosil RT 601, 9:1 ratio) poured into mold.


Fingertip Component Preparation

LED strip inserted into fingertip casing groove, placed upside down on 30mm hemispherical mold, and degassed transparent silicone gel poured for formation.


Curing Process

Both contact layer configurations cured at 60°C for 30 minutes to achieve optimal material properties.


Reflective Coating Application

Special mixture of Posilicone Translucent silicone (1:1 ratio), white pigment, and silver powder (2:0.1:0.05 ratio) applied as thin uniform layer and cured at 50°C for 20 minutes.


Final Protective Layer

Black paint mixture (Novocs Matte matting agent, Ecollex 00-10, black pigment in 26:6:1 ratio) uniformly airbrushed and cured at 50°C for 20 minutes.

Sensor Assembly Process

Step 1: Finger Skin Installation

Finger skin component secured by snapping into reserved slots on TPU framework sides. Integrated PVC membrane firmly fixed using six M1.6 screws and corresponding nuts.


Step 2: Fingertip and Light Integration

Fingertip component attached to TPU framework top via four mounting holes in connector piece. LED strip light passed through external light strip slots on TPU frame.


Step 3: Camera Mounting and Final Assembly

RGB camera mounted to finger bracket using screws and nuts. Fully assembled finger completed by sliding TPU frame into bracket grooves, ensuring precise alignment and secure fit.
